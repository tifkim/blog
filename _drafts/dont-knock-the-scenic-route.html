I was picking up my husband from work yesterday, and on my way there, I could see that the ride back home on our usual route would be miserable. There were cars lined up as far as the eye could see on this half-mile stretch to leave the office.

[caption id="attachment_18" align="aligncenter" width="300"] Route A[/caption]

The solution was simple. After he got in the car, we took a scenic route behind the office buildings that goes past a few community plots and farmland. This route is easily twice the distance but we still managed to get back within a reasonable amount of time and probably a little faster than if we'd sat in that line. Most importantly, we avoided the aggravating wait full of potholes and car-jerking to inch toward the exit.

[caption id="attachment_17" align="aligncenter" width="300"] Route B[/caption]

This, in itself, was just a small win on a regular day but it reminded me of something bigger I had just read about. In Laura Vanderkam's book I Know How She Does It, she discusses how negative incidences can drastically impact your perception of how many hours you work. The top professionals she worked with in her surveys documented working over 60 hours a week; however, when their activity logs were further inspected, they had actually worked closer to 30-40 hours. It's the anxiety, annoyances, and grievances that can really inflate your perceived amount of work.

This made me think a lot about why I might have been unhappy at my previous job. I think there were things that were both in and out of my control that made me feel overworked, and I'm still trying to better understand what those factors were so I don't repeat my mistakes. For the moment, I'm glad to take a break from that line of cars and enjoy the view.
